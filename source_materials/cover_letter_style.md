# Cover Letter Style Guide

## What makes a good cover letter from Chunxu

Reading all the letters written so far, the best ones share a few things:
- They sound like a real person wrote them, not a template
- They connect to the specific role with a concrete, honest reason
- They mention specific things (project names, people, situations) rather than vague claims
- They're short enough that someone actually reads them

The ones that don't work are the ones that start with "I am writing to apply for..." and spend three paragraphs restating the CV.

---

## Structure

Four paragraphs. Short. No bullet points.

1. **Why this role** — something specific that made you want to apply. Not "I am excited about" but a real observation, question, or moment of recognition. This is where personal stories go if Chunxu provides one.
2. **Technical evidence** — 1–2 concrete things from your background that are directly relevant. Name the project. Give a number if you have one. Explain what it taught you, not just what you did.
3. **Connecting paragraph** — why this role at this place, not just any job. What draws you to this team, sector, or problem specifically. This is where you show you've thought about them, not just yourself.
4. **Close** — one or two sentences. "I would be happy to discuss further" is fine. Don't oversell.

---

## Language rules

- No "I am passionate about" — show it, don't claim it
- No "I am excited to" — same problem
- No "leveraging my skills" — just say what you'd do
- No "I believe I would be a great fit" — let them decide
- No em dashes used decoratively — only where they genuinely aid clarity
- Vary sentence openings — not every sentence starting with "I"
- Concrete > vague: "40,000 samples" beats "large datasets"; "IsoformSwitchAnalyzeR" beats "a bioinformatics tool"
- Honest > impressive: saying "I'm early in my career but..." (like Nordic_Statistician) lands better than overselling

## Personal stories bank

These are real moments and situations from Chunxu's life that can be used as opening hooks or connecting paragraphs when relevant. Do not force them in — only use when they genuinely fit the role.

---

### Cancer & patient motivation
*Used in: Rigs.tex — the strongest personal opener Chunxu has written*

"In China, cancer is often spoken about indirectly, as it is closely associated with fear and loss. I have seen many patients and families share their situations publicly to raise funds for treatment, and whenever possible, I have tried to help in small ways. Over time, this also shaped how I think about my own work. I realised that, beyond sympathy, the most meaningful contribution I can make is through my skills."

**Use when:** Roles in cancer research, oncology, translational medicine, or anything where patient impact is the core mission.

---

### COVID-19 & public health
*Used in: SSI.tex*

"Having lived through the three years of the pandemic, I often think about how many lives might have been saved if we had been better prepared earlier."

**Use when:** Public health surveillance, infectious disease, genomic epidemiology roles.

---

### Re-applying to Nordic Bioscience
*Used in: Nordic_Statistician.tex — honest and direct, works well*

"Last summer, I applied for a master's thesis position but unfortunately was not selected. Since then, I have continued to follow the company and its work, and I have been looking for other ways my background might fit."

**Use when:** Applying again to a company where Chunxu applied before. Shows persistence without desperation.

---

### Moving to Denmark / international student experience
*Used in: DANSIC.tex — one of the more personal openers*

"Two years ago, I moved to Denmark with hopes of building a meaningful career in a country known as a European pharma hub. Now that I'm about to graduate and have begun looking for full-time jobs, the process has felt much more challenging than I expected."

**Use when:** Roles where the international/personal journey is genuinely relevant (e.g. organisations that work on diversity, inclusion, or international talent).

### DailyJobMatch / AI curiosity + traction
*Used in: BRIC.tex*

"To reduce the time spent on job searching, I developed an AI agent to fetch, filter, and rank job postings based on my CV. Through this process, I realised that AI becomes much more useful when it is customised and built with, rather than just a chatbot."

**Extended version with traction (use for PM / product / AI startup roles):**
DailyJobMatch got 5,000+ likes and saves on RedNote (Chinese social media) and 100+ GitHub stars — without any promotion. That response taught me something about what makes an AI product actually useful: it has to solve a specific problem for a specific person, not just demonstrate a capability.

**Use when:** Roles with an AI, LLM, or agentic component where showing genuine hands-on AI curiosity matters. Use the extended version when product sense or real-world adoption is relevant.

### Clinical research / thesis at Novo
*Used in: winter_school.tex*

"As a bioinformatics master's student, I work with patient-derived data often at a stage where clinical and regulatory decisions have already shaped what the data can show. I would like to better understand how those decisions are made."

**Use when:** Roles that sit at the interface of computational work and clinical development.

### Manager's question ("What is your baseline?")
*Used in: DANSIC.tex — good example of showing self-reflection*

"During a conversation with my manager at Novo, I mentioned that it feels like there are fewer opportunities for bioinformatics students, especially internationals. She asked me, 'What is your baseline and data source?' Then I realised that much of what I felt was based on impressions rather than evidence."

**Use when:** Roles that involve research methodology, evidence-based decision-making, or when wanting to show intellectual honesty.

---

## What NOT to do

- Don't open with "I am writing to apply for the [role] position at [company]."
- Don't write more than 4 paragraphs — it won't get read
- Don't list skills that are already on the CV — use the letter to explain motivation and fit
- Don't use the letter to apologise for gaps — address them briefly and move on
- Don't use any of the personal stories above if they feel forced for the role
